The gap exists because the simple mean difference contains bias.

Let's think about it intuitively using our tablet example.

To the claim "schools with tablets have higher test scores," you might counter:
"Those schools would have scored well anyway.
They had better funding, better teachers, and better facilities to begin with."
The two groups weren't comparable to start with.

In the language of potential outcomes, this means:
even without the tablet, the treated schools would likely have outperformed the control schools.

Let's verify this with math.
The observed association we can compute from data can be decomposed.

By adding and subtracting Y⁰ for the treated group, the simple mean difference splits into two parts.

One part is the actual causal effect among the treated — the ATT.
The other is the bias: how different the two groups were to begin with, even without treatment.

In other words: simple mean difference equals ATT plus bias.

Bias measures how much the groups differed in their baseline potential outcomes.
It arises because factors like school funding, location, and teacher quality
affect both who gets treated and what the outcome is.

Variables that influence both treatment and outcome are called confounders.

To estimate the causal effect correctly, we need to eliminate this bias.
That means the two groups should be comparable on average — everything except the treatment.

This is the central challenge of causal inference.
