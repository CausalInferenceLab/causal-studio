Let's verify this with real data.

We've been using the tablet example, but here we'll look at an actual experiment
studying how different teaching formats affect academic achievement.

Students were randomly assigned to one of three course formats:
in-person, fully online, or hybrid.
The format a student received had nothing to do with their individual characteristics.
Treatment is the course format; the outcome is the final exam score.

What would happen without randomization?
Students who choose online courses might be unusually self-motivated.
Or they might be students who can't commute due to financial hardship.
Either way, these differences would contaminate the results.
Randomization removes this bias by design.

Estimating ATE is then straightforward: just compare average scores across groups.

The in-person average is 78.55, and the online average is 73.64.
The estimated ATE is approximately negative 4.91.
Online instruction lowered scores by about 5 points on average.

We don't need to worry about whether online students come from lower-income backgrounds,
or whether they had weaker academic preparation.
Randomization handled all of that at the design stage.

Of course, we should verify that randomization actually worked.
Checking whether variables like gender and race are balanced across groups is good practice.
In this dataset, most variables look balanced.
The Black student variable shows some imbalance — but with a small sample,
random chance alone can produce these differences.
As sample size grows, such imbalances naturally decrease.
