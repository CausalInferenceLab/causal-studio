So when does association become causation?

Recall the decomposition: observed mean difference equals ATT plus bias.

For observed association to equal the causal effect, the bias term must be zero.

In notation, this means the expected Y⁰ for the treated group must equal the expected Y⁰ for the control group.

Intuitively: the schools that received tablets,
had they not received them,
should have scored about the same as the schools that didn't receive tablets.

In other words, the two groups must be comparable in everything except the treatment itself.

When this condition holds, bias disappears,
and the simple mean difference equals ATT.

Going one step further — if treated and control groups would respond similarly to the treatment,
ATT and ATC become equal.

And then the simple mean difference equals ATE as well.

To summarize: comparability is what turns association into causation.

One question remains: how do we actually create comparable groups in the real world?
