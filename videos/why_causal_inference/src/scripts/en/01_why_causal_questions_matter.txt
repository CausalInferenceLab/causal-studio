Why should we care about causal inference?

At its core, causal inference is about answering "what if" questions.

Consider an education policy decision.
With a limited budget, should we give every student a tablet?
Or would it be better to spend the same money on libraries and learning spaces?

The heart of this question is causal — not just descriptive.

And questions like this are everywhere.
Does higher education actually increase income?
Does raising cigarette prices reduce smoking rates?
Does a new ad campaign really drive more sales?

We encounter causal questions constantly, across every field.
Yet while "correlation is not causation" is a familiar saying, explaining exactly why is not so easy.
