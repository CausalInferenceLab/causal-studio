RCTs are simple and powerful — but they're not always possible in the real world.

The costs can be prohibitive, the design ethically impossible,
or the intervention too large-scale to control.
We can't randomly assign pregnant women to smoke, for example,
just because we want to know how smoking affects birth weight.

Even so, when approaching a causal question, it always helps to ask:
"If we could run the ideal experiment, how would we design it?"
That thought experiment becomes a benchmark for observational research.

This is where quasi-experimental methods come in.
We can't directly secure the independence between treatment and potential outcomes that an RCT provides —
but we can think carefully about which covariates to control,
and design our comparison to make treatment assignment look as good as random.

The methods ahead — matching, difference-in-differences, synthetic control,
instrumental variables, and regression discontinuity —
are each a different way of satisfying that core assumption.

Different tools, one goal: making groups comparable.
That single objective is the unifying thread running through all of causal inference.
