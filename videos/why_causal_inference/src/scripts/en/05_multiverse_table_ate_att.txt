To make this concrete, imagine we could jump across parallel universes and observe the counterfactual.

With that ability, suppose we collect data from four schools.
For each school, we can see both their score with a tablet and their score without one.

Treatment T equals 1 for the tablet condition, and Y is the test score as our measure of achievement.

Now that we can observe both Y¹ and Y⁰ for each school,
we can compute the Individual Treatment Effect directly.

For example, School 1's ITE is negative 50,
and School 3's ITE is negative 200.

ATE is the average of all these ITEs.
Add up negative 50, 0, negative 200, and 50 — then divide by 4 — and you get negative 50.

That means tablets, on average, lowered academic achievement by 50 points.

ATT is computed only for the schools that actually received treatment — those with T equals 1.
In this case, that's Schools 3 and 4.

So ATT is the average of negative 200 and 50, which gives negative 75.

Among the schools that actually got tablets, the average effect was a 75-point drop in scores.

The reason this calculation was easy is that we had access to both
the factual and counterfactual outcomes for every school.
