In this video, we'll explore RDD — Regression Discontinuity Design — one of the key methods in causal inference.

Let's start with an example to build intuition.
Suppose University A awards scholarships to freshmen who score 370 or above on the Korean CSAT, the Suneung.

A student who scored 369 and a student who scored 371 are just 2 points apart.
The student above 370 receives the scholarship; the other does not.
Yet we can't say there's a meaningful gap in ability between them.
That 2-point difference is far more likely the result of luck or how they felt on test day.

If we want to estimate the causal effect of the scholarship on academic achievement — on college GPA — what should we do?
How about comparing exactly these two students?
Since they differ by only 2 points, they are essentially equivalent in every way except for whether they received the scholarship. That makes them the ideal comparison pair.

This is the core idea of RDD — Regression Discontinuity Design.

When treatment is assigned based on whether a continuous variable crosses a specific cutoff, we compare units just on either side of that cutoff to estimate the causal effect.

Even without a randomized experiment, the situation near the cutoff naturally approximates random assignment — and RDD exploits that.

Beyond this example, there are more real-world settings for RDD than you might expect.
Scholarship eligibility, welfare benefit thresholds, legal drinking age, drug insurance coverage.

Wherever a clear rule determines treatment, RDD is worth considering.
