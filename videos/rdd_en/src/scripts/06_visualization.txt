Now let's build intuition through a visualization.

The graph gives us three layers of information:
a scatter plot, regression lines, and the discontinuous jump.

The scatter plot shows each student's exam score and college GPA.
Students who received the scholarship and those who didn't are color-coded.

The regression lines are fitted separately on the left and right of the cutoff.
The two lines are estimated independently.

The size of the discontinuous jump is the treatment effect.
We estimate it from the difference in intercepts between the two lines at the cutoff.

The core of RDD estimation is visually confirming how the regression lines differ on each side of the cutoff.
But there are a few important things to keep in mind when reading this graph.

As we keep emphasizing, causal interpretation holds only near the cutoff.
The fact that students scoring around 340 and those scoring around 390 have different GPAs may reflect differences in ability — not the scholarship effect.

Also, the slopes on the two sides are allowed to differ.
That is, the regression lines for the treated and untreated groups can have different slopes.
The relationship between exam score and GPA may vary depending on treatment status.
