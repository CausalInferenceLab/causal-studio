To understand RDD, let's review three core concepts from causal inference.

The first is the Running Variable.
It's a continuous variable that determines treatment assignment.
Exam scores, age, income level — each unit has a different value, and that value determines whether they receive treatment.
As we discussed with the assumptions, units must not be able to manipulate this value to select into or out of treatment.

The second is the Cutoff — the threshold that determines treatment.
In Sharp RDD, units at or above the cutoff receive treatment, and those below do not.

The third is Potential Outcomes.
Y-one is the GPA if the scholarship is received; Y-zero is the GPA if it isn't.
We can never observe both outcomes for the same student.
A student who received the scholarship reveals only Y-one; one who didn't reveals only Y-zero.

The cells marked with a question mark are the counterfactuals.
With 70 as the scholarship cutoff, RDD uses data near the cutoff to estimate the causal effect.

Note that RDD does not estimate the counterfactual for a student who scored, say, 90 points.
This is both a defining feature and, at times, a limitation of RDD.
