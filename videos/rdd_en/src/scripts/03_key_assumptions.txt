For RDD to produce valid causal estimates, two important assumptions must hold.
Let's use a new example: credit scores and mortgage loans.
Suppose a credit score of 700 or above qualifies someone for a mortgage, and we want to estimate the causal effect of that loan on home purchase.

The first is the continuity assumption.
Both the expected home purchase rate without the loan and the expected rate with it must vary smoothly near the cutoff — no sudden jumps.
If this holds, any sharp discontinuity in outcomes near the cutoff can only be attributed to the loan approval.

What happens if this assumption breaks down?
Suppose people with credit scores below 700 are eligible for public rental housing — meaning they don't need to buy a home.
But the moment someone crosses 700, they become eligible for a mortgage and simultaneously lose their rental housing eligibility.
In that case, we can't tell whether a rise in home purchase rates is because they can now get a loan, or because they lost access to rental housing.

The second assumption is local randomization.
This means units must be unable to precisely manipulate their running variable value.

For example, if a customer with a score in the 690s files a complaint with the financial regulator to force their score above 700 — or if a bank officer intentionally adjusts a client's score — local randomization is violated.
This is called manipulation, and it must be tested before any RDD analysis.
