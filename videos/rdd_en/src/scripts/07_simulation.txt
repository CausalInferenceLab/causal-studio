Let's verify this with a simulation.

We set the Korean CSAT scholarship cutoff at 370, with a true scholarship effect of 0.5 GPA points.
We'll check whether RDD can recover this effect.

We simulate data for 500 students.
Korean CSAT scores are drawn uniformly between 340 and 400.
GPA is generated as a function of the cutoff-centered score, plus the scholarship effect, plus some noise.

We fit separate regression lines on the left and right of the cutoff.
Then we estimate the treatment effect as the difference in intercepts at the cutoff.

The results show a left intercept of approximately two point zero two, and a right intercept of approximately two point five.
The estimated treatment effect is zero point four eight — close to the true effect of zero point five.

This simulation confirms that by fitting separate regression lines on each side of the cutoff, RDD can successfully recover the treatment effect.
