So how do we actually compute the Wald estimator with real data?

As mentioned, the Wald estimator is the change in the outcome divided by the change in treatment probability. A single ratio gives us an intuitive sense of the effect size.

Getting just the point estimate isn't hard — you compute the numerator and denominator separately and divide. The real problem comes next. We need a confidence interval to know how much to trust this value, but since the numerator's and denominator's errors are tangled together, getting the uncertainty of a ratio directly is tricky.

This is where we use 2SLS, two-stage least squares. Its point estimate is exactly the same as dividing the numerator by the denominator separately, but it also gives us a standard error and confidence interval.

By the way, 2SLS isn't specific to Fuzzy RDD — it's a general method for estimating the causal effect of an endogenous variable using an instrument.

In our Fuzzy RDD setting, eligibility is the instrument and treatment is the endogenous variable, which is exactly why we can apply this method.

As the name suggests, 2SLS has two stages. In the first stage, we regress treatment D on the instrument, eligibility Z. This gives us D-hat, the part of treatment that eligibility exogenously predicts — the part unrelated to the error term.

In the second stage, we regress the outcome Y on D-hat instead of the original D. This yields beta-hat-1, the treatment effect with endogeneity removed — this is exactly the causal effect, the LATE.
